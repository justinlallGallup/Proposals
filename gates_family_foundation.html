The Data That Census Figures and Program Evaluations Can't Provide

Federal data tells you how many people live in a community and what they earn. It doesn't tell you whether residents trust local institutions, where they get information about issues affecting their lives, what they experience when they try to access public lands, or whether they believe the next generation will have better economic opportunities than they did. These are the dimensions that determine whether a philanthropic investment succeeds or fails — and they are invisible in existing data.

Gallup uses address-based sampling to reach populations traditional research misses — including rural communities, tribal lands, and geographically isolated populations where response rates are lowest and the data gaps are widest. In Colorado, these are precisely the voices that would reveal where the greatest opportunities and blind spots are.

Fill Data Gaps

Probability-based methods that reach underrepresented populations — rural, tribal, geographically isolated — the communities most likely to be missed by traditional surveys and most important to hear from.

01
Detroit Resident Voices Survey — Gallup Center on Black Voices

Uncovering What's Working — in a Landscape Where No One Could See It

The Landscape

Detroit's Black residents experience some of the deepest racial equity gaps in the country. But no hyperlocal, city-specific data existed to tell leaders where disparities were worst, what residents needed, or which neighborhood investments were producing results. Without consistent data across a large swath of communities, philanthropy and government were investing without a map.

Gallup's Approach

Gallup partnered with the Detroit Regional Chamber and 50+ grassroots organizations to survey nearly 12,000 Black Detroiters — contacting 150,000+ households by mail with online response options. The survey covered economic opportunity, education, safety, health, and community environment at the neighborhood level.

What the Data Uncovered

The survey revealed something no existing dataset could surface: the Durfee area — a neighborhood with significant challenges — rated as the second-highest "thriving" area in the city. As Marnita S. Harris of the Detroit Regional Chamber wrote, a likely rationale was the presence of Life Remodeled's community hub — not only were resources available, but the investment made residents feel seen and heard. Without representative data, this finding — which could shape future investments from philanthropy, business, and government — would have remained invisible.

02
VoicesDMV — Greater Washington Community Foundation

A Regional Survey That Gave a Foundation the Evidence to Build a First-of-Its-Kind Coalition

The Landscape

In one of America's wealthiest metros, homelessness persisted and housing affordability was deteriorating. Philanthropy, government, and nonprofits needed a shared evidence base to align around common priorities and galvanize public support for action.

Gallup's Approach

Since 2017, Gallup has partnered with the Greater Washington Community Foundation to conduct VoicesDMV — a probability-based mail survey fielded every three years across the DC region, covering economic opportunity, wellbeing, safety, and democratic influence.

Tangible Outcomes

The survey revealed three-quarters of residents agreed ending homelessness was a priority and nearly two-thirds would pay more taxes for affordable housing. That data catalyzed the Partnership to End Homelessness — a first-of-its-kind public-private initiative with DC's Interagency Council on Homelessness.

03
Walk Free Foundation & ILO — Global Estimates of Modern Slavery

Survey Data Cited in Parliament and Written Into Law

The Landscape

50 million people were estimated to live in modern slavery. No nationally representative data existed to quantify the problem or drive legislative action.

Gallup's Approach

Gallup surveyed 77,000+ respondents across 72 countries through the Gallup World Poll. The data became the official U.N. SDG Target 8.7 baseline and was launched at the United Nations General Assembly.

Tangible Outcomes

In Australia, the data was cited during parliamentary debate and helped pass the Modern Slavery Act (2018). Companies now embed the findings in human rights due diligence and sustainability reporting. The ILO has since supported forced labor surveys in 17 countries using the methodology. The research has been cited in over 100 peer-reviewed publications.

04
Gallup-Purdue Index (now Gallup Alumni Survey)

The Data That Changed What Universities Optimize For

The Landscape

Higher education measured success through rankings and starting salaries — not whether the college experience prepared students for fulfilling work and life. No national framework connected what happens during education to long-term outcomes.

Gallup's Approach

Gallup built the largest representative study of college graduates in U.S. history — surveying 75,000+ alumni to identify six specific experiences that doubled graduates' odds of thriving. Only 3% had all six — exposing a massive, actionable gap.

Tangible Outcomes

The Indiana Commission for Higher Education adopted the framework statewide and added new scholarship requirements — mentorship and experiential learning — for its 21st Century Scholars. The entire UNC System (16 universities) surveyed 77,695 alumni and presented results to its Board of Governors. Robert Morris University wrote the findings into its institutional strategic plan.

05
World Bank Global Findex Database

The Survey That 110+ Countries Use to Set Financial Policy

The Landscape

Until 2011, no systematic way existed to track who had bank accounts or how billions of people managed financial risk. Policymakers relied on incomplete supply-side data.

Gallup's Approach

With funding from the Gates Foundation, the World Bank partnered with Gallup to build the Global Findex — the world's only demand-side survey of financial inclusion, fielded in 140+ economies every three years since 2011. It has been downloaded 100,000+ times.

Tangible Outcomes

Findex became the official U.N. SDG indicator (8.10.2) and is the definitive source informing national financial inclusion strategies in 110+ countries. Its disaggregated data exposed the gender gap in account ownership, catalyzing targeted investment — particularly in mobile banking across Sub-Saharan Africa.

The Opportunity

Colorado Resident Voices — What It Could Look Like

Gates Family Foundation invests across four interconnected domains — natural resources, education, community development, and community information. A statewide survey of Colorado residents would produce the first representative evidence base connecting these priorities through the lived experiences of the people the Foundation serves — especially those in rural, tribal, and geographically isolated communities that existing data misses entirely.

Below are two partnership approaches — each can stand alone or work together.

Option A: Colorado Statewide Resident Voices Survey

A probability-based, address-based mail survey reaching households across all of Colorado — urban Denver metro, rural Western Slope, mountain communities, Eastern Plains, and tribal lands. Designed to produce statistically valid estimates at the regional level, with oversampling in priority geographies. Covers natural resources, education, economic opportunity, community information, and housing. Fielded annually or biennially, with results benchmarked against national Gallup data.

Option B: Thematic Deep Dives + Qualitative Voices

Focused studies on specific populations or topics — forest economy communities, rural education access, water management attitudes, or housing affordability in mountain towns. Includes a quantitative survey component paired with qualitative recontact interviews that surface human stories, quotes, and themes. Produces the kind of narrative evidence that gives data emotional weight in policy conversations and media coverage.

Example: Questions That Can Only Be Answered Through Survey Research

Forest EconomyHow dependent is your household's income on natural resource industries — forestry, agriculture, ranching, or outdoor recreation?

Economic OpportunityHow confident are you that the next generation in your community will have better economic opportunities than you?
Economic OpportunityWhat is the single biggest barrier to economic mobility in your community — housing costs, job availability, transportation, childcare, or education access?
EducationDoes your community have adequate access to career and technical training programs that lead to jobs in your region's economy?
EducationHow much influence do students and families in your community have in shaping what schools teach and how they operate?
InformationWhere do you primarily get information about issues affecting your community — local news, social media, word of mouth, government sources, or community organizations?
InformationHow much do you trust the information available to you about decisions being made by local government and institutions that affect your community?

These questions measure dimensions that census data, administrative records, and program evaluations cannot capture — resident trust, perception of agency, barriers as experienced by those facing them, and the information ecosystems that shape how communities engage with the institutions investing in them. The answers would give Gates Family Foundation a shared evidence base for identifying where investments are producing returns, where the gaps are widest, and which strategies have the broadest community support to galvanize government and private co-investment.

Looking Ahead

Colorado's Communities Have the Answers — If We Ask the Right Questions

In Detroit, a survey uncovered that a neighborhood no one expected to be thriving was outperforming wealthier areas — because a specific investment model was working. In DC, a survey quantified that two-thirds of residents would support tax increases for affordable housing — giving a foundation the evidence to mobilize a $20 million coalition. In Indiana, survey data led a state to add mentorship requirements to its scholarship programs. In every case, the insight that changed strategy was invisible in existing data. It only emerged when someone asked the right questions of the right people.

Colorado faces a unique set of interconnected challenges — forest health and wildfire risk, rural economic isolation, housing affordability across mountain and metro communities, access to education pathways, water scarcity, and an information landscape where trust is eroding. These challenges cut across Gates Family Foundation's four strategic areas. A statewide resident voices study would connect them — producing the kind of representative, consistent, actionable evidence that lets leaders invest with confidence rather than assumption.

The communities furthest from Denver are the ones most likely to be missing from existing data — and the ones whose experiences would most reshape how Colorado invests.
