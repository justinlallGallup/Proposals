The baseline Gallup helped establish

Nearly a decade ago, Voices of Youth Count gave the nation its first credible national picture of youth homelessness. The findings reframed the conversation: roughly one in ten young adults ages 18–25, and at least one in thirty adolescents ages 13–17, experience some form of homelessness over the course of a year — about 4.2 million young people in all, once couch surfing and other hidden situations are counted alongside shelters and the street. The data showed that youth homelessness is just as common in rural America as in cities, and that it falls hardest on Black and Hispanic young people, LGBTQ youth, young parents, and those who never finished high school.

Those numbers unlocked federal attention, guided philanthropic strategy, and gave communities a shared baseline to work from.

Since then, the ground has shifted given the pandemic, stagnant wages, interrupted schooling, and a worsening housing market that have all reshaped the risks young people face. The nation needs a fresh, comparable national count.

The original estimates were derived from a survey module on the Gallup Daily Tracking survey, which is now retired given the shift away from telephone surveys nationally. Gallup is recommending an updated design that preserves the probability-based nature of the prior tool to ensure comparability in estimates despite some methodological changes.

What We Will Measure
A faithful continuation of the national prevalence and incidence study that preserves the original survey items, definitions, and age frame so that change over time can be measured cleanly.

A Consistent Instrument

We retain the original household screener and definitions — capturing the full range of homelessness for 13- to 25-year-olds, from sleeping in shelters, cars, or on the street to running away, being asked to leave, and couch surfing without a safe, stable place to stay. Where Chapin Hall wishes to refine specific items, we will calibrate any changes so that comparability with the original estimates is preserved.

Coverage of the Hidden & Hard to Reach

Youth homelessness is disproportionately rural, offline, and out of view. A probability-based design with a telephone component reaches households that online-only approaches systematically miss — the very families this study exists to count.

Trend Integrity Over a Decade

We explicitly engineer for comparability: matched wording, a probability-based foundation, a bridge to the original telephone survey, and weighting to federal benchmarks, with trend calibration relative to the 2017 baseline.

Methodology

The 2017 estimates were produced on Gallup’s national random-digit-dial telephone survey — a probability-based, dual-frame (landline and cellphone) instrument with live interviewers. That is the baseline any renewed count must be compared against. Our design preserves that lineage by combining two probability-based sources rather than an opt-in or fully online option, which will systematically underestimate the exact population we seek to cover.

01

Two probability-based sources, no opt-in dependence

Both the panel and the RDD frame are probability-based. The estimate never rests on self-selected respondents — a common source of bias in national prevalence figures.

02

A bridge to the original survey

Retaining a live-interviewer telephone component and probability-based methods lets us compare estimates directly to the 2017 results.

03

Coverage of the populations most at risk

Rural, lower-income, and offline households — over-represented in youth homelessness and under-represented online — are reached through the RDD frame, protecting the validity of subgroup estimates.

04

Screening at national scale

The RDD survey alone is a substantial probability-based effort, and roughly 32,000 household screens sit at or above the screening base of the original count — ample for stable prevalence estimates and for the rural and demographic breakouts the report depends upon.

05

Cost discipline by design

RDD is the more expensive frame. Concentrating it where it earns its cost while carrying the balance on the probability panel maximizes reportability and credibility while holding cost down.

Scope in Detail

Population & Definitions

  • Age frame: youth and young adults ages 13–25, reported via the household screener, consistent with 2017.
  • Homelessness, broadly defined: explicit homelessness (streets, cars, shelters, transitional housing) plus couch surfing — staying with others while lacking a safe, stable arrangement.
  • Measures: 12-month prevalence and incidence, plus the core characteristics and disparities the original report established.
  • Inclusivity: Spanish-language interviewing and all 50 states, preserving the reach of the original design.

Screening & Fielding

  • 20,000 household screens fielded in July 2026 through Gallup’s probability-based panel.
  • 12,000 RDD telephone screens at roughly 2,000 per month, dual-frame (landline + cell), live interviewers, multi-call design.
  • ~32,000 household screens in all — at or above the screening base of the original count — with those that screen in continuing to the full survey.
  • The majority of screening from the panel, anchored by a large probability-based telephone screen — a balance chosen to protect coverage and comparability while controlling cost.

Weighting & Integration

  • Dual-frame integration reconciling the panel and RDD screening sources into a single national estimate.
  • Weighting for unequal selection probability, nonresponse, and frame overlap, then to federal demographic benchmarks (CPS / ACS).
  • Mode-effect and trend calibration relative to the 2017 baseline.

Quality Assurance

  • Real-time field monitoring and validation across both sources.
  • Standing data-quality checks applied to the probability panel, consistent with Gallup’s published practice.
  • Instrument pretesting to confirm question wording performs as it did in the original count.
  • Full methodological transparency suitable for peer, funder, and federal review.
