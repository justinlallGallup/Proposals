The finding cut against the grain of a low-trust era: veterans remain among the most trusted people in public life. The work became an annual program — and part of the foundation for Rye Barcott's new book, Courage Can Save US.

With Honor's mission has never stopped at America's shores. Its work runs the length of the transatlantic alliance — delegations to Ukraine, support for the Baltic Security Initiative, and sponsorship of the London Defence Conference. On 13 October, Courage Can Save US publishes in London, carrying its argument — that principled service can renew democratic life — to a British audience.

And yet no comparable, repeatable measure exists of how the UK public understands courage, or of the trust earned by those who have served. This study creates one — running the core of the U.S. instrument for the UK, for a British read on the same questions, built to repeat rather than serve as a one-off snapshot.

What We Measure
A read on courage & values
The questionnaire is the base With Honor–Gallup instrument from the United States, refined for the UK — and ready to extend to allied NATO markets. It asks how the public sees courage today, where it sits among the values that hold a society together, and who is seen to live it.

The state of courage

Whether courage is seen as stronger or weaker than a generation ago — among the public, the young, and elected leaders.

Courage and the future

How essential courage and working across divides are to the country's future — and whether leaders pay a price for principled, unpopular stands.

The values landscape

Where courage sits among honesty, integrity, service, resilience and civility — and how present those values are in everyday people versus leaders.

Example items, carried from the U.S. instrument
  • “Compared with 20 years ago, is courage in British society stronger, weaker, or about the same?”
  • “How important is it for the country's future that elected leaders act with courage?”
  • “Which values were most important to the nation at its founding — courage, honesty, integrity, service, civility?”
  • “Is it a major problem when leaders choose not to stand up for what they believe is right?”
