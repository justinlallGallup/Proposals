To Results

For nearly 90 years, one name has defined the measurement of American public opinion. When George Gallup founded the American Institute of Public Opinion in 1935 and correctly forecast the 1936 presidential election against the prevailing wisdom of the day, he established a principle that still governs Gallup's work: that a scientifically drawn sample of ordinary citizens can speak, accurately and reliably, for the nation as a whole.

That principle lives on in the Gallup Poll Social Series — the recurring monthly survey Gallup introduced in 2001 to keep its long-term trends current. It is the instrument Gallup uses to update several hundred trend questions, some first asked in the 1930s, and to take the pulse of the country on the issues that matter most. Each month carries a different theme — from the environment in March to consumption habits and diet in July — and it is where Gallup already tracks how Americans eat, what they cut back on, and how they weigh their health against their impact on the planet.

There are billions of ways to live a life — and each one is worth studying.

The Instrument
What the Gallup Poll Social Series Is
A recurring monthly survey of U.S. adults, organized thematically across the year, built for consistency and long-term comparability.

Monthly & Thematic

Gallup conducts one GPSS survey per month, each devoted to a different topic and administered in the same month every year — a consistent standard that protects trends from question-order bias and seasonal effects.

Built for Trends

Introduced in 2001, the GPSS is the primary method Gallup uses to update several hundred long-term trend questions — some dating to the 1930s — while continually adding new items to address emerging issues.

The State of the Nation

Every survey carries the same core measures — presidential and congressional approval, national satisfaction, the job market, and the nation's most important problem — alongside extensive demographics.

Methodology
A Representative Sampling Frame
The Gallup Poll Social Series is fielded by live interviewers using a dual-frame, random-digit-dial design that reaches a representative cross-section of the U.S. adult population.
  • Dual-Frame Random-Digit-Dial
    Gallup samples both landline and cellphone numbers using random-digit-dial methods, reaching cellphone-only households and using a multi-call design to contact respondents not reached on the first attempt.
  • A Full National Sample
    A minimum of 1,000 U.S. adults aged 18 and older, living in all 50 states and the District of Columbia, interviewed by live interviewers in English and Spanish.
  • Weighted to the U.S. Population
    Samples are weighted to correct for unequal selection probability, nonresponse, and dual-frame coverage, then matched to Census targets for gender, age, race, Hispanic ethnicity, education, region, population density, and phone status.
  • Consistent, Comparable Design
    Core questions are asked in the same order each administration, allowing rigorous analysis of change over time that is not susceptible to ordering effects.

How It Works
From Your Questions to Published Findings
A guided, full-service process. You bring the questions; Gallup handles design, fielding, data, and reporting.
1
Submit Questions
You provide your draft items and research objectives. There is no need to arrive with finished survey language.
2
Gallup Refines
Gallup's methodologists apply best practices in survey instrument design — wording, response options, and placement — so your items measure what you intend.
3
Gallup Fields
Your items are added to that month's national GPSS survey and fielded to a representative sample of U.S. adults.
4
Cleans & Vets Data
Gallup cleans, weights, and vets the data, then builds full demographic crosstabulations against every item.
5
Reports Back
You receive your chosen deliverable — a topline summary or a Gallup.com article — with crosstabs included in every engagement.

The Monthly Cadence

Survey items are finalized in the third week of each month for the following month's survey — so environment questions can ride the March Environment poll, and food and diet questions the July Consumption Habits poll. Submitting early leaves room for Gallup's methodologists to refine your questions before the instrument locks.
