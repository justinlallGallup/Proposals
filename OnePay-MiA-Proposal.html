We made Lloyd's Register Foundation the global standard on safety. Money in America is how we do the same for OnePay.

Personal-finance coverage is saturated with the macro story — inflation prints, rate cuts, sentiment indexes. Almost no one measures the part that actually governs how people live: the day-to-day reality of money.

Americans don't experience the economy as a chart. They experience it as a balance on a Tuesday, a paycheck that lands two days early, a savings buffer that's either there or isn't, a quiet sense that they're building toward something — or just treading water. That lived experience is where trust, anxiety, loyalty, and financial behavior are actually formed. And at national scale, it is almost entirely unmeasured.

That gap is OnePay's white space. As an all-in-one platform built for how real people manage money — banking, saving, building credit, investing, and paying over time — OnePay is uniquely positioned to own the conversation about the everyday financial life of America. Not by talking about products, but by leading with people: what financial progress means to them, what gets in the way, and what "better money" actually looks like across income, generation, and place.

Done right, this isn't a one-time report. It's a repeatable annual moment — a credible, people-first body of evidence that compounds in authority every year, fuels evergreen content across PR, social, and marketing, and positions OnePay as the leading voice on the state of personal finances in America.

What Money in America Measures

Four dimensions of how Americans live with their money

A purpose-built instrument that moves past macro indicators to the lived reality — engineered to support clean subgroup reads by income tier, generation, and region.

01

Day-to-Day Financial Management

The daily mechanics and emotions of money — cash flow, control, timing, stress, and the small decisions that define financial life between paychecks.

In a typical month, how often does your money run short before more comes in?
02

Savings & Financial Resilience

The buffer beneath the surface — emergency savings, the ability to absorb a shock, and how protected or exposed people feel when the unexpected hits.

If you faced a sudden $400 expense, how would you cover it?
03

Financial Progress & the Future

Momentum and outlook — whether people feel they're building toward something, growing credit and savings, and the confidence they hold about what's ahead.

Compared with a year ago, are you moving forward, holding steady, or falling behind?
04

The Meaning of "Better Money"

The human definition of financial wellbeing — what progress means to real people, the goals behind the numbers, and what would genuinely change their lives.

What would having "better money" let you do that you can't do today?
BMI

Anchored by the Better Money Index™

A single, defensible composite score — fielded the same way every year — that turns the four dimensions into one trackable national number. The headline that makes this a recurring story, not a one-off.

Why Gallup

The findings only matter if the world believes them

A media moment is built long before launch day. It starts with data the press, academics, and policymakers actually trust — and a brand that can carry it into the rooms that matter.

One of only a few probability-based panels in America — and the largest

Most "national surveys" are stitched together from people who opted in online. The Gallup Panel is a different instrument entirely: members are recruited at random through address-based sampling and random-digit-dialing, so every person has a known chance of selection. It is not an opt-in list — it is one of only a handful of true probability-based panels in the country, and the largest of its kind. That is why a Gallup number can be projected to every U.S. adult and defended under any scrutiny, and why it carries weight a convenience sample never will.

Trust is the whole game — and it is getting scarcer

As AI-generated and fraudulent answers flood cheap online panels, major newsrooms have begun refusing them outright — ABC and The Washington Post among them. The bar for being taken seriously has risen, and Gallup sits on the right side of it. Trusted data gets covered, cited, and believed; convenient data gets ignored.
