Measuring Global Leadership & Credibility in a Shifting World

A multi-country public opinion initiative to understand how citizens around the world assess the credibility, reliability, and leadership of major global powers — and what that means for alliances, partnerships, and the rules-based international order.

Senator John McCain believed that America's greatest strength lay not in military might alone, but in its credibility — the willingness of allies and partners to trust the United States as a principled, reliable leader committed to shared rules rather than raw power. That conviction animated his decades of service and remains the foundation of the McCain Institute's work in the arena of democracy, human rights, and international leadership.

Today, perceptions of global leadership are shifting amid extraordinary geopolitical uncertainty. Ongoing conflicts in Europe and the Middle East, China's increasingly assertive economic and diplomatic posture, volatility in energy markets, and questions about the consistency of American engagement abroad are reshaping how publics around the world view major powers. These perceptions are not merely academic. They drive alliance behavior, economic alignment, and the willingness of governments and societies to cooperate on shared global challenges.

And yet, no comprehensive, repeatable instrument exists to measure these perceptions across countries, over time, with the rigor and independence the moment demands.

“Our interests are our values, and our values are our interests.”

Senator John McCain

The McCain Institute proposes partnering with Gallup to launch a Global Leadership and Credibility Survey — a comparative public opinion initiative designed not as a one-time snapshot, but as the foundation of an annual measurement program spanning five years. Rather than tracking surface-level favorability, this survey will explore the deeper architecture of global trust: which nations are seen as credible leaders, reliable partners, and responsible actors in the international system — and how those perceptions are changing over time.

Proposed Research Framework
What We Will Explore Together
The following dimensions represent Gallup's initial recommendation for the survey's core focus areas. Final research questions will be developed collaboratively with the McCain Institute team during the instrument design phase.

Global Credibility

Understanding which countries are viewed as credible, consistent leaders on the world stage — and which have seen their standing erode in the eyes of global publics.

Illustrative: “Which country do you view as the most credible leader in international affairs?”

Partner Reliability

Exploring the extent to which major powers are perceived as dependable allies and responsible stakeholders — nations others would count on in times of crisis.

Illustrative: “If your country faced a serious challenge, which global power would you most trust to help?”

Self-Interest vs. Common Good

Examining which powers are perceived as acting in the global interest versus those seen as pursuing narrow, transactional self-interest at the expense of others.

Illustrative: “Which major power acts most in the interest of the broader global community?”

Recommended: A Five-Year Annual Program

Gallup's experience with long-running global research programs — including the World Bank Global Findex and Wellcome Global Monitor — demonstrates that the most impactful public survey initiatives are built on multi-year cadence. Trend data becomes exponentially more valuable with each wave, and public release is most effective when audiences can track change over time. Gallup recommends an initial five-year commitment to maximize longitudinal value.

5-Year Term (Recommended)
Annual cost increases capped and discounted. Locked-in pricing for fieldwork and analysis through Year 5. Greatest longitudinal impact.
3-Year Term (Alternative)
Standard annual pricing. Three waves of data establish initial trends. Option to extend after Year 3.
Years 2–5 are streamlined: instrument development and refinement are replaced by a lighter instrument review and project management fee. No Wave 1 interim report — a single comprehensive global report each year.
