One Year of Data Is a Number. Two Is a Trend.

Year 1 gave the country its first rigorous measure of how scams reach American households. Year 2 turns that measure into a trend line — and turns a landmark report into the durable, annual record the field has never had.

In Year 1, Stop Scams Alliance and Gallup built something the field had long needed and never had: a nationally representative, methodologically rigorous measure of how scams actually affect American households. A survey of more than 5,100 U.S. adults — paired with in-depth interviews with fraud victims, a public report, a Gallup News article, an expert roundtable, and a research hub on Gallup.com — produced the most comprehensive baseline on scam victimization ever assembled in this country. The headline is sobering: an estimated $68 billion lost in 2025, roughly 15 million adults victimized, and emotional harm reported by nearly three-quarters of affected households.

That baseline is only as valuable as what comes next. The greatest risk now is that the data becomes a snapshot rather than a story — a single data point rather than a trend line. A one-time study establishes what the problem is. Annual tracking, fielded in the same season each year to remove seasonal noise, tells us whether things are getting better or worse, which populations remain most vulnerable, and whether interventions are working. No single study can do that.

The studies that move policy are not one-time reports. They become institutions — the Global Slavery Index and the Global Women’s Health Index, both built with Gallup, became the cited, ongoing measures in their fields. Scams in the United States is positioned to become that for fraud.

The step that gets us there is Year 2: repeating the study on the same schedule to produce the first real trend line. Because Year 2 builds on established instruments, infrastructure, and media relationships, it costs less than the inaugural study. At the same time, partner feedback has been clear that Year 2 is also the moment to sharpen the instrument and extend its reach — refining how scams are categorized, closing known blind spots such as the systematic undercount of total victimization, and exploring populations the field knows least about.

This proposal is built to make those choices fundable. It defines a core annual study as the base, presents specific expansions that a funder can choose to support independently, and accounts for the operating support that sustains the program. The configurator below lets each element be turned on or off so partners can see precisely what their investment delivers.

What Year 2 Adds
Sharper, Deeper, Broader
Year 2 retains the Year 1 core module in full — preserving comparability for trend tracking — while incorporating refinements and expansions surfaced through partner and funder feedback.

A Trend Line

The Year 1 core module is retained so prevalence, scam types, platforms, demographics, reporting behaviors, and financial and emotional impacts can be tracked year over year, fielded in the same season for valid comparison.

Preserves: full comparability with the Year 1 baseline.

Higher Resolution

Clearer categorization of scam typology and origin, richer detail by scam type (average loss and demographics), and the larger samples needed to report reliably at the level of individual scam experiences.

Closes gaps: systematic undercount and under-reported scam types.

New Populations

Optional dedicated samples reach audiences the field knows least about — teens aged 13–18, fielded through Gallup’s existing Gen Z panel, and small-business owners — each with a purpose-built module.

Extends reach: youth and small business victimization.
Approach
Built on Year 1, Designed to Repeat
Gallup conducts the second annual cycle on the proven Year 1 foundation — the probability-based Gallup Panel, an iterative design process, and the same seasonal window — so that what changes in the data reflects the world, not the method.
01
Instrument Refinement
Design workshop and stakeholder input to refine the core instrument, sharpen scam categorization, and add new and updated items — without breaking trend comparability.
02
Cognitive Testing
Cognitive interviews to confirm clarity, reduce respondent burden, and validate new items before fielding, following Gallup’s standard pre-field protocol.
03
Fielding
Web administration via the Gallup Panel to a nationally representative sample, fielded in the same season as Year 1, with real-time quality monitoring.
04
Analysis & Trend Report
Weighting, subgroup analysis, and year-over-year trend analysis, delivered as a 30-page public report, a Gallup News article, and a refreshed research hub.

Why Gallup
The Infrastructure to Make It Annual
A continuous tracking program needs a partner built for exactly that — probability-based sampling, a standing Gen Z panel, decades of attitudinal research, and a global news platform to carry the findings.

Probability-Based, Built to Repeat

For more than 85 years, Gallup has led public opinion research. The Gallup Panel — the largest probability-based panel in the U.S., with more than 100,000 adults recruited via random-digit-dial and address-based sampling — keeps results representative year after year, and a standing Gen Z panel makes reaching teens feasible. Findings can be carried by Gallup’s own news service, which reaches a global audience and is regularly featured in leading outlets, giving the research the visibility it needs to drive change.
