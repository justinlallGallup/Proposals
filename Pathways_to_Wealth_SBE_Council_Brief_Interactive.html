  • Perception: for non-owners, lack of capital is the top barrier to starting (45%); 40% say "not wealthy enough"
  • Among those who seriously considered it: capital (49%) and wealth (45%) lead the list
  • The "complicated" part: most actual owners don't seek capital — only 10% did in 2023; just 3% applied for a loan
  • Why not: 47% don't want debt; 45% can cover expenses without outside funding
  • Capital is a scaling tool: owners who hired recently were 3× likelier to seek capital and 6× likelier to apply for a loan (24% vs. 8%)
  • Credit is the gatekeeper: 75% of owner-employers report excellent credit vs. 51% of non-employer owners
  • And it predicts performance: median profit $10K (excellent credit) vs. $1K (less-than-good); revenue ~5× higher
  • Local support helps: 41% of owners who got a loan or grant grew revenue vs. 24% who didn't — though direct financial support is rare
  • Takeaway: most don't need capital at launch (they rely on savings); external capital mainly matters for hiring/scaling firms; strong credit is the key to getting it

Adoption & value

  • Owners out-adopt employees: ~38% of owner-employers and 37% of self-employed used an AI chatbot for work in the last 30 days, vs. 30% of employee-only
  • Context: across the broader Gallup workforce (Q4 2025), 46% of U.S. employees use AI at work at least occasionally; 26% frequently
  • Owners self-direct adoption — little formal training or strategy; employer-provided tools mostly go to employee-only staff at large firms (13%)
  • Owners rate AI "highly useful" more than employees (every owner type); almost none call it "not useful"
  • Top owner uses: general inquiries/research (29%), emails/proposals (27%), product/service creation (21%), solving customer problems (19%), plus marketing and accounting/tax

Jobs

  • Net positive so far: 4% of owner-employers say AI led to layoffs; 10% say it helped them hire more
  • Counter-signal: 17% of employee-only workers say AI led their org to avoid new hiring (larger firms)
  • Stakes: small businesses = 55% of net U.S. job creation, 2013–2023

The Gallup point of view

  • Adoption happens through people, not tools — the #1 predictor of org adoption is a manager championing it (8.7× likelier to say AI transformed their work); in a small business, the owner is that manager
  • AI is an amplifier of existing management quality — owners with strong management practices benefit most; management fundamentals = the best AI-readiness program
  • Macro caution: despite ~$40B in enterprise AI spend, ~95% of orgs report no measurable profit impact and ~89% no productivity effect — but small owners, closer to their problems, may be better positioned to capture value
  • Where AI can unlock scaling: cutting the admin load of becoming an employer — payroll, compliance, benefits, bookkeeping, customer management

04 Talking points

How to run the conversation.

Five moves, in order. Each opens to the full context behind it.

Capital access is the issue most associated with Karen — she helped build the crowdfunding framework that became the JOBS Act — so it is the natural place to start, and the clearest signal that our research speaks to her life's work. Lead with the reframe from the Year 2 report: capital is the single biggest barrier in the minds of people who have not started a business (45% cite it), yet most actual owners do not pursue outside financing — only 10% sought capital in 2023, and most say they either don't want debt or don't need it. The real constraint is not appetite; it is creditworthiness, which gates both whether owners can secure financing and how their businesses perform. That nuance — that the capital story is more complicated than "entrepreneurs need more money" — is exactly the kind of evidence that resonates with someone who has spent three decades on capital-access policy.
This is the freshest material and the strongest common ground. Small business owners are out-adopting traditional employees on AI — roughly 38% of owner-employers used an AI chatbot for work in the last month versus 30% of employee-only workers — rate it "highly useful" more often, and are using it to grow rather than cut: among owner-employers, 10% say AI helped them hire more people while just 4% report layoffs. That mirrors SBE Council's own technology survey, which lets Stephanie note that two independent research operations are landing on the same story. Beyond the numbers, the Gallup point of view is worth sharing: AI adoption happens through people, not tools. The single biggest predictor of whether an organization adopts AI is whether a manager actively champions it — and in a small business, the owner is that manager. The practical takeaway for an organization that serves entrepreneurs is that management fundamentals are effectively the best AI-readiness program, because AI amplifies whatever systems a business already has.
SBE Council has been publishing on small business succession in recent weeks — the employer-versus-nonemployer question specifically — and we hold the hard longitudinal numbers, including that 27% of employer firms with owners 55 and older are unsure of their plan or expect to close. It is an active concern for them and a concrete, obvious candidate for joint work, so it is worth naming as a place where our data could directly support their agenda.
One of the more counterintuitive results is that owners feel far better about their local environment than the national one — 47% are highly satisfied locally even as national sentiment runs negative — and that what drives local satisfaction is community infrastructure: peer-to-peer networks, permitting and local regulation, and city services, more than tax rates or real estate costs. For an advocacy and policy organization, that is actionable: the levers that matter most to entrepreneurs are ones cities and local institutions can actually pull. It also reinforces the AI point, since owners adopt through trusted peer networks rather than top-down programs.
Position any collaboration as complementary strengths — Gallup brings the rigorous, original, longitudinal measurement her audiences trust, while SBE Council brings reach into the entrepreneurial community and a policy voice — and keep the "where might we work together" question open so she can shape it. End on wellbeing: the study is as much about wellbeing as wealth, a theme Karen cares about personally and professionally, and a reminder that this research speaks to more than the balance sheet.
