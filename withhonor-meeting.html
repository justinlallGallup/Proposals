01 About With Honor

A cross-partisan bet on veteran leadership

With Honor exists on a simple premise: principled veteran leadership can lower the temperature of American politics and strengthen democracy — and veterans are one of the few groups the public still trusts across party lines.

With Honor recruits, trains, supports and elects principled veteran leaders who commit to serving with integrity, civility and courage — including a pledge to work across party lines. It is built around a theory of change: the cross-partisan trust veterans still command can be converted into public leadership that is less performative, less polarized, and more oriented toward country, service and problem-solving.

The broader ecosystem includes political and advocacy arms (With Honor Action), while With Honor Institute is the 501(c)(3) nonpartisan nonprofit focused on research, education, convenings, fellowships and evidence-based work on democracy and polarization. The Gallup partnership runs through the Institute. That is precisely why Gallup matters to them: it supplies credible, nonpartisan evidence that the public still sees veterans as unusually trusted civic actors — letting With Honor move from assertion to data.

The footprint is real. After the 2018 cycle, With Honor–backed veterans co-founded the For Country Caucus — now roughly 37 members of the U.S. House, united not by ideology but by shared service and a commitment to country over party. Looking to the 2026 midterms, With Honor is tracking 474 verified veteran candidates in federal races, with veteran candidacies expected to rise on both sides of the aisle.

The Principal

Rye Barcott

Co-Founder & CEO, With Honor · U.S. Marine Corps veteran

Barcott's path blends military service, civic entrepreneurship and democracy reform. He served in the Marines in Iraq, Bosnia and the Horn of Africa. Before With Honor, he co-founded Carolina for Kibera, a community-development organization in the Kibera settlement of Nairobi, and wrote about that work and his service in It Happened on the Way to War. His graduate study at Harvard — and the Kennedy School's "Profiles in Courage" tradition — shaped a lasting interest in political courage. His public message isn't simply that veterans should hold office; it's that the country needs leaders with the courage to put country over party, build trust across difference, and take principled stands even when there's a political cost.

02 The relationship

From one custom module to a five-year platform

A short history in five phases. Select a phase to see the scope, the deliverables and what each step unlocked.

Phase 1 · July 2025

Launch & design — trust in veterans

The engagement was formalized in July 2025 around a focused question: how do Americans view veterans, and do they see military service as a meaningful qualification for leadership? Year 1 was built around a custom veteran-leadership survey.

  • A custom ~2-minute module (5–8 items), fielded to a nationally representative sample of ~2,000 U.S. adults on the Gallup Panel.
  • Gallup led survey design, data collection, analysis, toplines, a Gallup.com article and communications support — tied to With Honor's October 2025 gala.
Phase 2 · September–October 2025

First public release — veterans as trusted leaders

Announced September 3, 2025 as the first collaboration and a landmark study on public trust in veterans. The findings — "Military Experience Tops Candidate Credentials" — were released around Veterans Day.

  • Positioned the work as the start of a broader effort to understand how Americans perceive veterans' role in society.
  • Established the candidate advantage (55% more likely) and a leadership profile: country-before-self, ability to work across difference, credibility in high-stakes settings.
  • Fielded Aug 1–15, 2025 (n=2,132); amplified through op-eds and partner coverage.
Phase 3 · December 2025

Benchmark integration — Honesty & Ethics

The relationship moved into Gallup's longitudinal public-trust infrastructure. Veterans were added to Gallup's annual Honesty & Ethics ratings (and the Confidence in Institutions track).

  • Dec 1–15, 2025 poll; veterans debut at 67% — #2 behind nurses, ahead of doctors and pharmacists.
  • The clean talking point: veterans aren't just respected in a custom study — they rank among the most trusted groups in a benchmark tracked for decades.
  • Converts a one-time finding into a recurring, trendable signal.
Phase 4 · March 2026

The expansion — America250, values & courage

A change order added a five-year standalone public-opinion research program, broadening the inquiry from trust in veterans to the values veterans — and With Honor's mission — are meant to represent: courage, integrity, service, civility, unity and principled leadership.

  • An annual ~30-item, ~7-minute survey: a recurring core module plus rotating annual topics.
  • Gallup Panel fielding at n≈3,000; weighted, nationally representative results with demographic breakdowns.
  • Year-over-year tracking beginning in Year 2, with annual co-branded deliverables.
  • Themes span moral and physical courage, unity, national pride and optimism — framed around whether the values that made the country possible are still alive, and who's carrying them forward.
Phase 5 · June 2026

Public values releases — renewal & courage

The first expanded releases, both drawn from the May 12–22, 2026 survey, connected With Honor's leadership mission to the country's 250th anniversary.

  • The America250 article (June 17) — pride, connection and optimism as the nation approaches 250.
  • The Courage release (June 24) — the courage gap between home and Washington, and the cost of principled leadership.
  • Timed with Rye Barcott's bestselling Courage Can Save US, giving the data a human, cross-partisan face.

What the new survey actually measures

The 2026 work isn't a single courage question — it's a full values architecture, moving from how the country feels to where courage and unity actually live.

The moral-courage items — mapped to With Honor's mission

"Doing what you believe is right even when it's difficult, unpopular, or costly."

That's how the survey defines moral courage. The specific behaviors it tests are, almost line for line, the things With Honor asks its leaders to do:

Speaking up when someone is treated unfairly
Admitting when you are wrong
Disagreeing publicly with your own side
Standing by a decision despite personal attacks

03 Key talking points

"Americans trust veterans; Americans believe courage is declining; Americans want leaders who'll act with courage across divides. With Honor sits at the intersection of all three."

1Where it started

Our partnership began with one question: do Americans still see veterans as trusted civic leaders? They do. Military service is the single strongest positive credential we tested — 55% more likely to vote for a veteran, just 7% less — and Americans credit veterans with strong leadership, putting country over party, and working across differences.

2The benchmark

We then gave that trust a permanent home. When we added veterans to Gallup's Honesty and Ethics ratings for the first time, they came in second only to nurses, at 67%. In a year when most professions hit record lows, veterans are one of the few groups that still carry cross-partisan credibility.

3The evolution

The work has grown from a veterans-and-trust study into a multi-year platform on civic values and democracy. The question now isn't only whether Americans trust veterans — it's whether the country still has enough courage, integrity, service and civility in public life, and who can carry those values forward.

4The headline

Americans haven't lost faith in courage — they've stopped seeing it in Washington. 62% see courage in their own communities; only 30% in their elected leaders. That 32-point gap is the story, and it's exactly the gap With Honor exists to close.

5Essential but costly

Americans overwhelmingly say courage matters — 79% call it vital for leaders — yet 76% believe leaders are punished for taking principled, unpopular stands. That's a direct opening for With Honor's message: the country needs leaders willing to absorb those costs.

6Not partisan

The strongest signal in the data is that the appetite for courage and principled leadership cuts across party lines. Americans disagree on plenty, but they broadly agree leaders should stand up for what's right and work across the aisle even when it's risky.

7The 250th frame

As the country turns 250, the research asks whether the values that made America possible are still alive and who's carrying them forward. Two-thirds plan to mark the milestone, 96% feel pride, and 88% see reason for optimism — the most-cited reason being people willing to stand up for what they believe.

8Why Gallup

Gallup is what lets With Honor move from assertion to evidence — a nonpartisan, methodologically rigorous way to talk about trust, veterans, courage and democracy. That credibility is the strategic value of the relationship in a low-trust environment.

The headline finding · June 2026

Americans still see courage. They just don't see it in Washington.

Sixty-two percent say people in their own communities act with courage. Just 30% say the same of elected leaders — a 32-point gap. Asked which groups they most associate with courage, Americans name first responders and military personnel first; elected leaders rank near the bottom.

It is, almost exactly, the gap With Honor exists to close.
